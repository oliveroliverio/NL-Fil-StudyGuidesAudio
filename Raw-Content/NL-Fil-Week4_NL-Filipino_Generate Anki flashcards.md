# Loan Words

### Filipino Loan Words (Mga Hiram na Salita)

**Introduction**

For almost 400 years, the Philippines was under the political domination of powerful countries, the most prominent among which were Spain and the United States. These countries left marked imprints on the Filipino culture‚Äîwhich is best expressed through language. But even before the mercantilistic endeavors of the 16th century, and along the way while the two big powers were in domination of the people and the country, other peoples and cultures have been in regular interaction with Filipinos (Scott, 1984). It comes as no surprise, therefore, that the languages and dialects in use in the country today bear cultural traces of these interactions.

Words which are adopted from a foreign language with minimal or no modification are called **loan words**. Philippine languages and dialects are heavy with loan words, largely from Spanish and English. Modifications of the original words, if at all, are mostly on spelling to fit the words into the phonetic and graphemic systems of the native languages. Below are examples of Filipino loan words and the changes that they went through.

**Greeting & Leave-taking (Pagbati at Pagpapaalam) Common Expressions**

When you meet or leave someone, here are some expressions you will most likely find useful:

- **Magandang umaga.** - Good morning
- **Magandang tanghali.** - Good noon.
- **Magandang hapon.** - Good afternoon.
- **Magandang gabi.** - Good evening.
- **Magandang umaga naman.** - Good morning, too.
- **Magandang hapon din.** - Good afternoon, too.
- **Magandang gabi rin.** - Good evening as well.

- **Magandang umaga, Tanya.** - Good morning, Tanya.
- **Magandang hapon (po), Ginoong Ramos.** - Good afternoon, Mr. Ramos.
- **Magandang hapon (po), Binibining Cruz.** - Good afternoon, Miss Cruz.
- **Magandang gabi (po), Ginang Arenas.** - Good evening, Mrs. Arenas.

---

- **Kumusta?** - What's up?/How are things going?
- **Ayos lang.** [**OK lang**] - Just fine.
- **Kumusta ka?** - How are you?
- **Mabuti naman. At ikaw?** - Fine. And you?
- **Kumusta po kayo, Ginang Arenas?** - How are you, Mrs. Arenas?
- **Mabuti naman.** - Fine.
- **Kumusta ang pamilya mo?** - How is your family?
- **Mabuti naman sila, sa awa ng Diyos.** - They are fine/well, with God's grace.

- **Paalam po, Ginang Arenas. Hanggang bukas.** - Goodbye, Mrs. Arenas. See you tomorrow./Until tomorrow.
- **Paalam. Mag-ingat ka.** - Goodbye. Take care.
- **Bay, Tanya. Hanggang mamaya.** - Bye, Tanya. See you later.
- **Adyos. Ingat ka.** - Goodbye. Take care.

---

The following are Nouns of Address in Filipino:

- **Binibini (Miss) + ng + Last Name** = **Binibining Cruz** (or Bb. Cruz when written)
- **Ginoo (Mister; Sir) + ng + Last Name** = **Ginoong Ramos** (or G. Ramos when written)
- **Ginang (Mrs.)** = **Ginang Arenas** (or Gng. Arenas when written)

**Ikaw & Ka - 2nd person pronoun, singular (You)**

- Use **Ikaw** at the beginning of a sentence or clause; use it in a Subject + ay + Predicate sentence structure. This is the general rule. **Example: Ikaw ay mag-ingat (You take care.)**
- Use **Ka** after a verb, an adjective, or as an expression of greeting; use it in a Predicate + Subject structure. **Example: Mag-ingat ka.**

**Kayo ‚Äì 2nd person pronoun, plural (You) OR to address one person only to show respect**
